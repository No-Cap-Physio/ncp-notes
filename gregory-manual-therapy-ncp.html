🧠Core Thesis
The Goal Is Winning, Not Return to Sport
  • Every table treatment must directly improve the athlete's ability to handle sport demands
  • Clinical question: not "what is wrong?" — "what limits this athlete's ability to perform?"
  • Return to sport is the floor, not the ceiling
Shrink the Feedback Loop
  • Treatment without immediate movement feedback is guesswork
  • Pick-Through method: treat → retest movement → treat → retest — each pass gives you data
  • Trackside/courtside treatment removes the telephone game between injury, clinic, and training floor
You Cannot Out-Treat Bad Coaching
  • Treatment nudges athletes toward better mechanics — coaching reinforces it
  • Without both, neither works long-term
  • The hip is the central driver — treating it resolves a disproportionate share of knee, ankle, back, and shoulder complaints
Pliability Is the Key Functional Test
  • Compress a muscle — if the athlete cannot actively move through it, they cannot handle loaded movement in sport
  • This is the difference between passive range and functional tissue quality
  • Pass = tissue available under load. Fail = treat before loading.
🔑Key Concepts
1. Pick-Through / Performance Therapy
  • Quick table treatment applied at the training site, not the clinic
  • Treat one area → retest movement → treat next area
  • Treatment budget: 90s–2.5 min trackside — 5 min max before coaches object
  • 5 min clinic still leaves 25 min for relationship, load management, history
  • One Treatment Task: one pass, one area, retest — this is the core skill to develop
2. Aberrant vs. Protective Tension
  • Aberrant tension: within the range of motion, restricts movement before end range — treat this
  • Protective tension: last ~20% of range — normal, leave it alone
  • No tension / looseness: also a problem — instability, don't mobilise more
  • Weak muscles feel tight when first loaded — that's DOMS, not dysfunction
  • Training fixes DOMS; treating it blocks adaptation
3. Pain Is Not the Target
  • Never ask athletes what hurts — never use the word "pain" in session
  • Movement is the measure — use tension quality and whether movement changes after treatment
  • "If shit moves better, you did a good job."
  • General population still needs pain addressed — but always use movement change as the primary guide
4. Stiffness as a Performance Quality
  • Stiffness = contact the ground → load through the kinetic chain → drive from the hip → pop off
  • Calf/ankle-dominant push-off = stiffness deficit = Achilles problems, posterior knee pain, bunions, claw toes
  • Ground reaction force in sprinting: 8–12× BW
  • Heel still touches the ground at top speed — Achilles only handles this if the whole chain shares load
  • Glute hop test: if glutes aren't engaging at each hop, athlete is calf-dominant, not hip-driven
5. Layer-Based Treatment
  • Surface tissues must be released before you can access deeper structures
  • Shortening a muscle gives access to deeper layers underneath
  • Knee bent = gastroc goes slack, soleus exposed
  • Toes curled = FHL exposed — three tissues, one hand position, ~1 minute
6. Long Lever Muscles Dominate the Shoulder
  • Muscles farthest from the shoulder joint have the most leverage over it
  • Small tightness in the lat creates large tension at the shoulder
  • Priority order: Lat (king) → Pec major/minor → Tricep long head → Teres major
  • Deltoid tightness "rarely, rarely matters" — don't start there
7. Hip Power Drives Shoulder Performance
  • In throwing, lifting, golf, basketball — hip rotation restriction → shoulder compensates for velocity deficit
  • MLB research: each joint distal-to-proximal adds to terminal velocity
  • Hip deficit (−200 m/s) → shoulder takes the load
  • "80% of rotator cuff issues in weightlifting are a hip problem"
8. Stimulus-Based Rehab
  • The final rep closest to failure is the stimulus — not the total volume before it
  • One true failure = one training session worth of adaptation stimulus
  • Table-side: compress target muscle → athlete contracts against your thumb → fail → done
  • One stimulus every 10–14 days is sufficient if the failure was real
  • For trained athletes: the sport IS the rehab — don't add extra volume
⚠️Nuances, Caveats & Red Flags
Treating an Unstable Athlete Aggressively = Making Them Worse
  • Real case: daily treatment made a top-10-Asian 100m sprinter worse
  • 3 months off treatment + 2–3 min performance therapy 2–3×/week resolved everything
  • Screen for stability BEFORE loading treatment
Stability Masquerading as Mobility Restriction
  • Athlete tightens into rotation = motor control/stability deficit, NOT tissue restriction
  • Do NOT mobilise aggressively in this case
  • Strengthen in that range first, then re-assess
Big Toe Hypermobility
  • Very loose big toe passive ROM = compensating for insufficient hip extension or ankle plantarflexion
  • Don't treat the toe — treat the hip and ankle
MRI Timing
  • No treatment to an area for ~1 week before MRI
  • Acupuncture creates inflammation that shows as swelling on imaging — distorts the structural picture
  • If athlete improves with washout period alone, you already have your answer
Post-Treatment Soreness
  • 2–4 days expected for aggressive treatment on long-restricted tissue
  • Swelling/fullness is also normal — warn athletes in advance
  • Subscap: max once every ~2 weeks — very bruisy, not a weekly treatment
⚑ Speculative / Clinical Experience Only
  • Pliability test, layered tissue concept, hyperpolarisation language — all clinically plausible but not formally validated
  • "From clinical experience. It's definitely not Cochrane."
  • Treat as high-quality clinical intuition, not peer-reviewed evidence

🔄Feedback Loop & Pick-Through Method
Pick-Through Flow
  • Step 1: observe movement (A-walk, kick-out, squat, sport-specific drill)
  • Step 2: note what you see — write it down before treating
  • Step 3: one treatment, one pass → retest same movement
  • Step 4: did it change? Yes → next limiter. No → wrong area, try again
  • Total trackside budget: 90s – 2.5 min
The Telephone Game Problem
  • Real case: athlete → "bench press caused chest pain"
  • Trainer → said no bench press / Coach → confirmed push-ups done after training
  • Three different stories about the same event from three people
  • Trackside treatment removes this — treat what you see, when you see it
📐Dynamic Movement Assessments
A-Walk / A-Skip Screen
  • Primary pre/post treatment proxy for ankle and hip function
  • Look for: foot turn-out, heel height at top, push-off origin (hip vs calf vs toe), pelvic oscillation
  • With 1080 or resistance band — instantly makes the drill a rehab mechanism
  • Pelvic oscillation = normal and desirable. A DROP on one side = the problem.
  • Pelvic drop = stance leg ankle stability failure on the SAME side (not weak hip on the dropping side)
Glute Activation Hop Test
  • Hop in place, place hands on glutes, feel for glute contraction with each landing
  • Pass: strong glute contraction every hop = driving from hip = stiffness present
  • Fail: no glute engagement, push-off felt in calf/knee = stiffness deficit = Achilles/posterior knee risk
Weightlifting Squat Screening Battery
  • Pistol squat: basic single-leg loading assessment (back rounding allowed)
  • Shrimp squat: hip locked in extension — tests true hip flexion under load. Fail = cheating through the back. Fix: deep step-ups, single-leg box squat
  • Cossack squat: knee wide, hip shifted over = adduction + IR. Tests posterior hip expansion. Fix: hip airplanes
💪Table-Side Rehab & Stability
Contraction Under Compression (Pliability / Activation)
  • Compress target muscle with thumb → athlete contracts against pressure
  • Can contract: muscle is pliable and active — move on
  • Cannot contract: muscle is hyperpolarised / inhibited
  • Do activation reps: contract → relax → repeat until contraction gets smooth, faster, stronger
  • Works for: subscap, hip flexor, VMO, adductor magnus, glutes
  • Effectively the same stimulus as motor point acupuncture — just with your thumb
Failure-Based Muscle Stimulus
  • The last rep closest to failure is the stimulus — not the total sets/reps before it
  • One true failure = one training session worth of adaptation stimulus
  • Rest period before re-stimulus: 10–14 days if the failure was genuine
  • For trained athletes: their existing training is sufficient — don't add extra volume
Grip Strength Standards for Manual Therapy
  • Minimum to treat without injuring yourself: 40–45 kg
  • Target for athlete population: 60–80 kg
  • Target for large/loaded athletes: 100 kg
  • Speaker's current: ~80 kg (formerly 110 kg)
  • Team China staff average: ~22 kg — necessitates needling and tools

🦶Ankle Decision Tree
Assessment Order Before Loading
  • Step 1 — Big toe: MTP flex freely? Stiff or hypermobile?
  • Step 2 — Subtalar: can the calcaneus rock into pronation/supination?
  • Step 3 — Gastroc/Soleus: posterior chain clear?
  • Step 4 — Lateral: peroneal strength and control present?
  • Step 5 — Medial: tib post and arch integrity intact?
  • Clear all five before loading the ankle chain in any sport context
Big Toe Triangle Release
  • Three points around the big toe MTP joint
  • 3–4 reps per spot → neurologic reset of MTP mobility
  • Critical for any athlete with bunion pattern, claw toes, or restricted push-off
  • Don't mobilise if hypermobile — find the cause (hip extension deficit, ankle plantarflexion deficit) and treat there instead
Subtalar Scoop — "Unlocking the Foundation"
  • Hold the calcaneus and gently scoop/rock it into pronation
  • 3–5 reps per session
  • If the subtalar doesn't unlock, none of the downstream ankle joint functions work
  • Athletes who can't pronate → overuse big toe → MTP hypermobility → bunion loading → eventual MTP breakdown
🦵Posterior Lower Leg — Layered Approach
Same Hand, Three Layers
  • Layer 1 — Gastroc: knee straight, toes neutral
  • Layer 2 — Soleus: knee bent, toes neutral (gastroc slack)
  • Layer 3 — FHL: knee bent, toes curled (deepest tension exposed)
  • All three muscles get ~3× the stimulus vs treating each individually
  • Total time: ~1 minute for all three layers in one hand position
Soleus Isolation Test
  • Ask for heel rise → observe
  • Manually hold heel → ask same contraction
  • Activation only with manual heel support = athlete is pushing through toes, not driving calcaneus up
  • Soleus isn't working → treat and then retrain calcaneal drive pattern
🦶Lateral Leg — Steering Mechanism
Peroneal Isolation Test
  • Push foot into eversion → measure resistance
  • Curl toes → re-test eversion resistance
  • Significant strength loss with toes curled = compensating with toe extensors, peroneus is inhibited
  • Implication: ankle instability, foot twisting in push-off, bunion loading
Tibialis Anterior Treatment
  • Find the tibia bone, push TA tendon off of it
  • Plantarflex → twist foot inward (inversion)
  • Key area: along the tibia shaft, not just muscle belly
  • Clears: anterior ankle impingement, improves clean dorsiflexion
Peroneus Treatment
  • Start at tib-fib joint level
  • Work between peroneus and tib ant — don't jam straight down (nerves on lateral side)
  • More stretch technique, less compression force
  • Work from tip of fibula up the full peroneal length
🦵Popliteus — Magic Button
Why It's a Magic Button
  • Releasing here addresses: tibial IR, tibial extension, inner hamstrings, medial gastroc, pes anserine, popliteal nerve complex
  • Indication: knee extension pain, ACL post-op, meniscus, MCL, medial knee pain, high calf pain, can't stay upright in single-leg work
  • One of the highest-yield release points for any lower limb athlete
Finding and Treating
  • DO NOT push midline (vessels + tibial nerve)
  • Stay lateral to medial gastroc, aim to touch tibia — it's deep, commit to the depth
  • Lateral approach: on the bone medially, scoop in, feel popliteus tendon on tibia
  • Hold until pain reduces to 50%, then progress to knee extension while holding
  • Acupuncture: cleanest needle point here — no vessels on bone approach

🏋️Hip Philosophy
The Hip Drives Everything
  • "If there's anywhere I'll spend most of my time treating — it's the hip."
  • Treating the hip well resolves a disproportionate share of knee, ankle, back, and shoulder complaints
  • The hip is the primary force transfer zone between trunk and lower limb — restriction creates compensatory load in both directions
  • Triple extension myth: only occurs once per race — at start push-off. During top-end speed, athletes are stacked, not in full extension.
  • Coaching athletes into extreme hip extension during top-speed running → hamstring tear risk
🏋️Anterior Hip — Treatment Sequence
Landmark Map
  • ASIS → find the hole just medial/inferior → in this hole: anterior capsule, rectus femoris tendon, sartorius, TFL
  • Lateral to medial: TFL → sartorius → pectineus → inguinal ligament → iliacus → inner pelvic bowl
  • Key rule: find the most restricted structure — treat only that one, not all of them every session
1 — Gentle Hip Flexor Release
  • Supine, find inguinal ligament, just above it at oblique line level
  • Low thumb/fist pressure, walk the leg away slowly
  • Use for: pain-sensitive athletes, elderly, first pass on any athlete
2 — Psoas Release (Shortened Ab Position)
  • Supine, posterior pelvic tilt (pillow under lumbar) — shortens abs, gives deeper access
  • Enter at belly button level, move toward inguinal ligament
  • Athlete pushes knee into hand (hip flexion resistance) to engage psoas
  • Rotate leg side to side (small rotation) while pressing down — stretches psoas at vertebral origins
  • Best for: low back spasm, hyperextension posture athletes
3 — Iliacus-QL Combined Release
  • Supine, knee bent, leg resting on therapist's thigh
  • Go lateral to psoas → corner of iliac crest → inner pelvic bowl → iliacus
  • Athlete pushes knee into therapist's leg → iliacus engages
  • Physically rotate your torso (elbows) to bring the whole spine through — separates iliacus from QL
  • Best for: SI joint pain, low back spasm, post-heavy lifting
4 — Sidelying Iliacus Stretch
  • Sidelying, both hands find iliacus and stretch apart
  • Athlete reaches arms and legs to fold around therapist
  • Best for: basketball, volleyball, racket sports, javelin — full extension-rotation pattern
6 — Hip Capsule / FAI Technique ⚠️ Advanced Only
  • Only after ALL soft tissue work is done and pinching still persists
  • Athlete rotates leg into IR, pulls knee to chest
  • Therapist thumb over ASIS/pectineus area as fulcrum — thigh folds over thumb → gaps anterior-superior joint
  • Athlete controls rotation — therapist guides direction, does NOT twist knee
  • Contraindicated: recent MCL injury, hip replacement, patient intolerant
🏋️Posterior Hip — Glute / Piriformis
Greater Trochanter Pass
  • All the way around the greater trochanter — releases all tendon attachments at once
  • Palpate for tightest spot → hold → bring knee to chest
  • Neurologically more efficient than chasing individual muscle bellies
Piriformis — Partner Version (Preferred)
  • Midpoint between greater trochanter and sacrum
  • Partner assists leg to 90°, guides into external rotation while therapist maintains pressure
  • Indication: proximal hamstring pain, SI joint pain, pain at bottom of squat
  • Solo version significantly less effective
🏋️Groin / Adductor Sequence
Adductor Magnus Separation from Hamstring — KEY TECHNIQUE
  • Fall off adductor longus medially → feel gracilis → pass underneath → diagonal up toward femur
  • Leg in abduction + IR → athlete extends knee
  • Purpose: separate adductor magnus from hamstrings — they jam up and restrict both hip extension AND knee extension
  • Adductor magnus = powerful hip extensor — undertrained and undertreated in almost every sport
  • Train with: deep step-ups, RDL, split squat at full depth, lunges
⚡Motor Point Acupuncture / E-Stim
Target: Monoarticular Muscles Only
  • Only muscles crossing one joint: VMO, adductor magnus, popliteus, brachialis, short supinator, foot intrinsics
  • When inhibited, they hyperpolarise — slower to contract, delays reaction time by milliseconds
  • Top 3 needle targets for hip/knee: VMO, adductor magnus, popliteus
  • Resting membrane potential: −72 mV — hyperpolarised = takes longer to fire

🦾Shoulder Philosophy
Long Lever Muscles Are the Priority
  • Muscles farthest from the shoulder joint create the most tension at the shoulder
  • Small tightness in the lat = large effect at the shoulder joint
  • Priority order: Lat (king) → Pec major/minor → Tricep long head → Teres major
  • Deltoid tightness "rarely, rarely matters" — don't start there
The Hip Powers the Shoulder
  • In any throwing, lifting, swinging, or racket sport — hip rotation restriction → shoulder compensates for velocity deficit
  • "80% of rotator cuff issues in weightlifting are a hip problem"
  • Fix hip first, then shoulder — this is the correct sequence
Fixed Points Enable Shoulder Movement
  • Open-chain shoulder movement requires fixed proximal points
  • Cervical spine and thoracic spine must stabilise for the shoulder to create force
  • A loose neck = trap takes over = shoulder mechanics collapse
  • Treatment order: thoracic and neck before shoulder joint
🦾Lat Treatment
Lat — Tricep Tendon Entry
  • Find tricep tendon (long head), trace it proximally
  • Lat sits just anterior to it, intertwined — get between tricep tendon and lat, pin and stretch
  • Raise arm overhead through full range while holding
  • Purpose: dissociate lat from teres, allow scapula to upwardly rotate freely
Lat — Inferior Scapular Border
  • Find inferior border of scapula — pull skin away first before pushing down
  • Hold inferior border, athlete raises arm overhead
  • Instant restriction often felt — lat is holding the scapula down, preventing upward rotation
  • After release: scapula should glide up freely as arm rises
Lat — TL Junction (T12 Area)
  • At lower ribs, off the edge of the erectors at T12 level
  • Key magic spot: affects QL, low back, trunk rotation, breathing, shoulder function simultaneously
  • Hold here, arm goes overhead, then shift arm around
  • Affects: T12/L1 facet rotation, QL, lower ribs, rib flare patterns
Lat — Lower Armpit / Serratus Release (Sidelying)
  • Sidelying, pull shoulder blade up (or knee under to support)
  • Go under the lat, over the serratus, along the ribs — knife-edge technique
  • Never push into ribs — always along the contour of the rib cage
  • Feel for: scapula adhered to rib cage (tissue bridges going across)
  • As arm raises overhead, fingers should feel scapula gliding under them
🦾Pec & Subscap Treatment
Pec Release — Three Anchor Points
  • Anterior deltoid: hold down, externally rotate, work through
  • Under collarbone (subclavius): proximal third of clavicle, go down, find pec head. Hold clavicle with one hand, push pec with other → externally rotate and row down. Peels pec from clavicle. Key for weightlifters with "jammed back" in front rack.
  • Pec minor (along ribs underneath): go along rib from below, push up into it, pull off to side
Finding Subscap — "Yoga Mat on the Floor"
  • When fingers are on subscap: soft, squishy (yoga mat) with hard bone underneath
  • Only feel soft muscle, no hard surface = not deep enough, go deeper
Subscap — Contraction Test First
  • Get onto subscap → ask athlete to internally rotate against your thumb
  • Can push you off → contracting — move to release
  • Cannot contract → do activation reps: push in, athlete contracts, relax, repeat until strong
  • This IS the treatment for weak subscap before doing manual release
Subscap Frequency ⚠️
  • Max once every ~2 weeks — very bruisy
  • For athletes post-snatch/jerk: quick 10-second version to "open the shoulder" before a set
Subscap Stability Test
  • Athlete: elbow down, internally rotate, hold position
  • Therapist: try to lift athlete off table by this arm
  • Pass: athlete stays locked in, whole body comes up together
  • Fail: athlete folds / elbow goes up → subscap cannot stabilise
  • Use for: rotator cuff stability assessment, instability cases, overhead sport athletes
🦾Neck & Upper Trap
Key Rule: Be Very Gentle with the Neck
  • Cervical spine is protecting the most important part of the body
  • Aggressive neck treatment causes guarding — the opposite of what's needed
  • "With aggressive athletes, they can't work the next day if you go too hard."
Upper Trap Myth
  • Upper trap is demonised as "always tight and bad"
  • Reality: it must have tension because it's fighting gravity all the time
  • In many athletes it's actually too weak — gravity overworks it, or it compensates because the shoulder can't glide well
  • Treat to offload and let smaller scapular muscles work — not to eliminate tension entirely

🖐️Wrist Treatment
Wrist Assessment Logic
  • The wrist is very mobile — many joints can be the pain source. Big 3: Lunate, Scaphoid, TFCC
  • Thumb side: strength and force production issues
  • Pinky side (TFCC): strength elements of the wrist
  • Rotation test: hold wrist, twist — if wrist torques instead of staying locked = rotation isn't working — this is the primary finding to treat
Pronator Quadratus Release — Primary Technique
  • Fingers under flexor carpi ulnaris, vice grip technique, push across
  • Hold pronator quadratus while actively supinating the wrist
  • Start with rotation only → then add wrist extension → then add fingers — never all at once
  • Very intense — described as "miraculous" for TFCC pain and crushing grip pain (baseball, golf)
Wrist in Barbell Sports
  • Straight bar forces fixed rotation — if forearm rotation compromised, torque goes to wrist
  • Short-term: switch to dumbbells. Long-term: restore forearm rotation.
  • Front rack: if tricep/lat don't release, elbows can't come up, weight sits on wrists instead of deltoids
Tennis / Golfer's Elbow
  • Go to shoulder first — check external rotation
  • Restricted shoulder ER → athlete pre-cocks wrist to fake rotation → overloads elbow repeatedly
  • Fix shoulder rotation first, then address the elbow
🏃Acute Hamstring Management
Spasm vs. Tear — The Key Distinction
  • Feel a dip on palpation: tear → protect, ice immediately, do NOT load
  • Feel firmness but no dip: likely cramp/spasm → offload treatment, keep moving, cool down
  • Many "hamstring tears" in sprinters are actually cramps at max speed — heals much faster if managed correctly
Acute Offload Protocol
  • Wiggle the leg to loosen, then long-axis traction (hip into therapist, gentle motion)
  • Groin/adductor gliding — take tension off everything connecting to the hamstring
  • Work through the calf and popliteus area — take tension off the distal end
  • Palpate the hamstring for a dip before going near it
  • Work ALL AROUND the injured site — not on it. Let the area breathe.
  • After every pass: recheck — more motion? Good. Flaring? Stop.
Ice Timing
  • Immediate ice is high value — drops swelling fast
  • After the initial window (10–20 min × 2 bags), shift to blood flow strategies
  • Don't keep icing — you need vasodilation and blood flow to flush the area
Timeline Reference
  • True spasm = can return to running in 5–10 days
  • Spasm at top speed = still looking at ~8 weeks because surrounding structures took the impact
  • Cool down: ~20 min bike, light jog, blood flow work after acute management
🦵Plantar Fascia
Densification Concept
  • Italian research (fascia): hyaluronic acid fills dense fascial layers under repeated compression → pressure, heat, friction
  • Densification threshold: ~3 min 40 sec of sustained treatment before tissue begins to release
  • Can't do quick passes and expect real change — must work through the full time window
Plantar Fascia Protocol (Indirect)
  • Work the tibial nerve first: curl toes → take slack → go back to normal → work along tibial nerve path
  • Back of knee (popliteal area): same neural offload technique
  • Piriformis: compress and move off
  • Offloads the nerve contribution to pain without touching the plantar fascia directly
Metatarsal Head Mobilisation
  • Adjacent metatarsal heads: push one up, one down → flip → repeat along all heads with restriction
  • Mobilises plantar fascia along all metatarsal attachments simultaneously
  • Combined with tibial nerve offload = ~80% of cases respond well

Treatment without immediate movement feedback is guesswork
Pick-Through method: treat → retest movement → treat → retest — each pass gives you data
Trackside treatment removes the telephone game between injury, clinic, and training floor
Treatment nudges athletes toward better mechanics — coaching reinforces it — without both, neither works
The hip is the central driver — treating it resolves a disproportionate share of knee, ankle, back, and shoulder complaints
Pliability test: compress a muscle — if athlete cannot actively move through it = cannot handle loaded sport movement
Passive range and functional tissue quality are not the same thing

Key Concepts & Numbers
Treatment budget: 90s–2.5 min trackside — 5 min max before coaches object
5 min clinic still leaves 25 min for relationship, load management, history
Aberrant tension: within range, restricts before end range — treat this
Protective tension: last ~20% of range — normal, leave alone
No tension / looseness = instability — don't mobilise more
Weak muscles feel tight when first loaded — that's DOMS, not dysfunction — training fixes it, treating it blocks adaptation
Never ask athletes what hurts — movement is the measure
"If shit moves better, you did a good job."
Ground reaction force in sprinting: 8–12× BW — entire chain must share load
Calf/ankle-dominant push-off = stiffness deficit = Achilles problems, posterior knee pain, bunions, claw toes
Surface tissues must be released before accessing deeper structures
Knee bent = gastroc slack, soleus exposed. Toes curled = FHL exposed.
Long lever muscles (Lat, Pec, Tricep LH) dominate the shoulder — small tightness in lat = large tension at shoulder
Hip rotation restriction → shoulder compensates for velocity deficit: −200 m/s hip deficit → shoulder takes the load
"80% of rotator cuff issues in weightlifting are a hip problem"
One true failure = one training session worth of adaptation stimulus
Muscle re-stimulus frequency: 10–14 days if the failure was genuine
For trained athletes: the sport IS the rehab — don't add extra volume

Day 1 — System
Champion Builder / Pick-Through / Assessments
Pillar 1: Find the Limiter — what actually prevents performance, not what the diagnosis is
Pillar 2: Create the Plan — broken → podium, not broken → return to sport
Pillar 3: Modify Training — keep training stimulus while managing injury
Pillar 4: Use the Sport as Rehab — sport drills ARE the rehab mechanism
Telephone game case: athlete → trainer → coach = 3 different stories about the same injury event
A-Walk screen: foot turn-out, heel height, push-off origin (hip vs calf vs toe), pelvic oscillation
Pelvic drop = stance leg ankle stability failure on the SAME side — not weak hip on the dropping side
Glute hop test: hands on glutes, hop — strong glute contraction every landing = pass. No contraction = calf dominant.
Shrimp squat: hip locked in extension. Fail = cheating through the back. Fix: deep step-ups, single-leg box squat.
Cossack squat: tests posterior hip expansion. Fix: hip airplanes.
Pliability test: compress muscle → athlete contracts against pressure → cannot contract = hyperpolarised → activation reps
Reactive stability: push athlete while they hold plank/bear. Pre-comp = stimulate, not sedate.
Grip strength — minimum: 40–45 kg / athlete: 60–80 kg / large: 100 kg / Team China avg: ~22 kg
Day 1 — Foot & Ankle
Ankle Decision Tree + Posterior Leg + Popliteus
Ankle decision tree order: big toe → subtalar → gastroc/soleus → lateral (peroneal) → medial (tib post)
Big toe triangle release: 3–4 reps per spot around MTP joint — neurologic reset
Subtalar scoop: hold calcaneus, rock into pronation, 3–5 reps — if this doesn't unlock, nothing downstream works
Athletes who can't pronate → overuse big toe → MTP hypermobility → bunion loading → MTP breakdown
Layered calf: knee straight = gastroc / knee bent = soleus / toes curled = FHL. One hand, three layers, ~1 min
Each layer gets ~3× the stimulus vs treating individually
Soleus isolation test: activation only with manual heel support = athlete pushing through toes, not calcaneus
Peroneal isolation test: eversion strength drops with toes curled = peroneal inhibition (toe extensors compensating)
Tib ant treatment: push TA tendon off tibia. Plantarflex → inversion. Work along tibia shaft. Clears anterior ankle impingement.
Peroneus treatment: start at tib-fib joint. More stretch, less compression. Work from tip of fibula upward.
Popliteus = magic button: releases tibial IR, tibial extension, inner hamstrings, medial gastroc, pes anserine, popliteal nerve
Popliteus: stay lateral to medial gastroc, aim for tibia. Hold until pain reduces to 50%, then progress to knee extension while holding.
Day 1 — Hip
Hip Treatment Sequences
"If there's anywhere I'll spend most of my time treating — it's the hip."
Triple extension: only once per race (start push-off). Top-end speed = stacked, not extended. Coaching extreme hip ext at top speed → hamstring tear risk.
Anterior hip landmark: ASIS hole → TFL → sartorius → pectineus → inguinal lig → iliacus → pelvic bowl
Key rule: find the most restricted structure — treat only that one, not all of them every time
Psoas release: posterior pelvic tilt shortens abs → deeper access. Enter at belly button, move toward inguinal lig. Rotate leg side to side while pressing. Best for low back spasm.
Iliacus-QL release: rotate torso (elbows) to separate iliacus from QL. Best for SI joint pain, low back spasm, post-heavy lifting.
Sidelying iliacus stretch: athlete reaches arms and legs to fold around therapist. Best for basketball, volleyball, racket sports.
FAI technique: ONLY after all soft tissue work done. Contraindicated: recent MCL, hip replacement, intolerant patient.
Greater trochanter pass: all the way around = releases all tendon attachments at once. More efficient than chasing individual muscle bellies.
Piriformis: midpoint between GT and sacrum. Partner version significantly more effective than solo.
Adductor magnus separation: fall off add longus → under gracilis → diagonal toward femur. Leg in abduction + IR → athlete extends knee. Separates add mag from hamstrings.
Adductor magnus training: deep step-ups, RDL, split squat at full depth, lunges
Motor point / e-stim target: monoarticular muscles only (VMO, add mag, popliteus, brachialis, foot intrinsics)
E-stim: 1 Hz = post-op/swelling / 2 Hz = early rehab / 10 Hz = neuromuscular re-ed / High = spasm
Resting membrane potential: −72 mV — hyperpolarised = slower to fire, delays reaction time by milliseconds
Day 2 — Shoulder
Shoulder Treatment System
Priority order: Lat → Pec major/minor → Tricep LH → Teres major. Deltoid "rarely, rarely matters."
Fix hip first, then shoulder — hip restriction = shoulder overload at end of kinematic chain
Treatment order: thoracic and neck before shoulder joint
Lat — tricep tendon entry: get between tricep tendon and lat. Raise arm overhead while holding. Dissociates lat from teres.
Lat — inferior scapular border: hold inferior border, arm overhead. Scapula should glide up freely after release.
Lat — TL junction (T12): key magic spot. Affects QL, low back, trunk rotation, breathing, shoulder function simultaneously.
Lat — lower armpit/serratus: knife-edge along ribs. Never push into ribs. Feel for scapula adhered to rib cage.
Pec release — 3 anchor points: anterior deltoid / under collarbone (subclavius) / pec minor along ribs
Subscap: feels like yoga mat on floor — soft and squishy with hard bone underneath. If no hard surface = not deep enough.
Subscap: always do contraction test first. Cannot contract = do activation reps. Max treatment frequency: every ~2 weeks.
Subscap stability test: whole body comes up together = pass. Elbow goes up = fail (subscap cannot stabilise).
Neck: be very gentle. Aggressive treatment causes guarding — the opposite of what's needed.
Upper trap: in many athletes it's too weak — overworked by gravity or compensating for poor shoulder glide. Don't eliminate tension entirely.
Day 2 — Wrist / Hamstring / Plantar Fascia
Wrist, Hamstring & Plantar Fascia
Wrist assessment: rotation test = hold wrist, twist — if torques = rotation isn't working. Primary finding to treat.
Pronator quadratus release: fingers under FCU, vice grip, push across. Start rotation → then extension → then fingers. Never all at once.
Wrist in barbell sports: straight bar forces fixed rotation — if forearm rotation compromised, torque goes to wrist. Fix = restore forearm rotation.
Tennis/golfer's elbow: fix shoulder ER first. Restricted shoulder ER → athlete pre-cocks wrist → overloads elbow repeatedly.
Hamstring: feel a dip = tear → protect and ice. Feel firmness but no dip = likely spasm → offload and keep moving.
Acute hamstring: work ALL AROUND the site, not on it. Groin gliding + popliteus/calf work to offload both ends.
Ice: 10–20 min × 2 bags immediately, then shift to blood flow. Don't keep icing.
Hamstring spasm return: 5–10 days (true spasm) / ~8 weeks (spasm at top speed)
Plantar fascia: fascial densification threshold = ~3 min 40 sec — must sustain treatment through this window
Plantar fascia protocol: tibial nerve offload + metatarsal head mobilisation = ~80% response rate. Never treat the fascia directly first.
